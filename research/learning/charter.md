# Learning Research — Charter

Tracks what makes students actually learn and retain, so the game is designed
on evidence rather than gamification folklore. Findings translate into
mission-design rules ([content-pipeline.md → learning-science design rules](../../docs/wiki/content-pipeline.md))
and schema/engine features. Run a pass with the **`/learning-research`**
skill. Shared rules live in the [research index](../README.md).

## Tracks

| # | Track | What to watch |
|---|---|---|
| L1 | **Learning science & behavioral psychology** | retrieval practice, spacing, feedback timing, desirable difficulties / productive failure, motivation (self-determination theory, intrinsic vs extrinsic gamification), retention & transfer |
| L2 | **Pedagogy for novice programmers** | cognitive load theory in computing education, worked examples, Parsons problems, subgoal labeling, misconception research, scaffolding by self-efficacy |
| L3 | **AI in education — evidence & products** | LLM tutoring efficacy studies, adaptive-learning outcomes, notable product mechanics worth learning from, market adoption (context only) |

## Stream-specific methodology (higher source bar)

- **Meta-analyses and systematic reviews first**; single studies only when no
  synthesis exists, and flagged as such. Strong outlets: Educational
  Psychology Review, Psychological Science, npj Science of Learning, ACM
  ICER/TOCE/SIGCSE, Computers & Education, Review of Educational Research.
- Blogs and vendor trend reports are **leads only, never evidence**.
- Every design recommendation must name the **effect it relies on and its
  approximate effect size** (e.g. "spacing, g ≈ 0.28 in math meta-analysis").
- Each finding ends in a **design implication for this game** — which engine
  feature, schema field, prompt rule or wiki design rule it touches. A finding
  with no consequence for the product is a footnote.
